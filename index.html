THE ALGORITHM

HOW IB'S RESPONSE TO COVID-19 HURT LOW-INCOME STUDENTS

The Core Values

The school culture centers around 3 community values: Corazon (Heart), Justicia (Justice), and Crecimiento (Growth).

To and Through College

One of KIPP's central goals is to push its students to and through college. The IB program is one way the school pursues this goal.

The IB Program

Developed mostly for wealthy boarding schools, the IB program now offers college-level courses to a diverse range of high schools.

Located on the south side of San Antonio, TX, KIPP UPrep is a public charter school that serves predominantly low-income students. It's claim to fame: the IB Program.

Once a standardized curriculum for elite boarding schools, International Baccalaureate (IB) provides college-level courses to high school students across the world. KIPP UPrep is one of the world's only low-income-serving high schools that enrolls students, by default, in the IB program.

In a normal year, students complete coursework, a major project, and sit for a culminating IB exam. But 2020 was, of course, different. Unable to administer exams due to COVID, the IB program instead used an algorithm to assign each student a final score.

After getting their scores, student and families wondered: was this algorithm fair?

IB scores are assessed on a 1-7 scale. If a student scores a 4 or higher, they earn college credit. In a normal year, every IB class has a project and an exam (each graded on the same 1-7 scale) that count towards their final grade. In addition, teachers assign students a predicted grade (also 1-7) that doesn't affect student scores.

In 2020, IB cancelled its exams. Instead, the program used an algorithm to impute student final scores from the projects (which had already been submitted), teacher predicted grades, and historic data.

Globally, the algorithm was "kind" to students, boosting scores on average. However, at KIPP, scores were lower. Were the 2020 KIPP students lower-performing, or did the algorithm hurt their scores?

Economically disadvantaged students have performed worse than global averages in previous years. This raises several questions:

  • Is IB using historic data in a way that lowers student scores at KIPP by default?
  • Are students at KIPP particularly hurt by taking project and predicted grades in place of exams?
  • Or could it be that the KIPP Class of 2020 was actually just lower-performing than the Class of 2019?

Across performance levels, the algorithm hurt KIPP students - especially in subjects with good exam scores.


In 2020, across the score distribution, far fewer students received scores higher than their project/predicted grades. In addition, more students received scores below their project/predicted grades. Filtering by courses with typically high exam scores magnifies the trend.

Unlike their high-income peers, low-income students often can't afford the help of a tutor. They often struggle to complete projects while working full time. So, their project grades are low. The exam provides them a better opportunity to show their learning. Although a failing project grade is a good predictor of failing the exam for wealthy students - at KIPP, a failing project grade is the norm, but some students still crush the exam. The algorithm, unfortunately, could not pick up this nuance. It imputed scores based on the global curve. KIPP students' scores suffered.

Clearly, scores trended downwards.

There seems to be bias when comparing a school that serves predominantly low-income students (KIPP) to other IB schools. Yet, algorithmic bias between students within the same school may also be a concern. So, we investigated.

However, when slicing the data within KIPP by gender, race-ethnicity, and economic disadvantage status, there are no clear biases across years. This may be why we haven't seen many news stories about IB's algorithm. It's easy for students and families to compare scores within schools (where there's no evidence of bias). It's much harder to compare results between schools - where there is clear reason for complaint.

Advocate.





By taking project grades and teacher predicted grades in place of exam scores, it's clear that IB's algorithm hurt students at KIPP University Prep. Unlike their higher income peers, the students at KIPP were not given a fair shot at earning college credit.
